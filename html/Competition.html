LATEST NEWSLETTER


  • Propulsion Sub-Team

    This year, the Propulsion team has been hard at work ironing out flaws in our designs and procedures. We started with the small Phobos-1 engine that had been machined last year, and managed several hot-fire tests with it. Although the data was inaccurate due to incomplete fuel burning, we learned a lot about fuel and graphite manufacturing that we fully expect to be applicable to Phobos-2 and -3. We also finished designing Phobos-2 and the larger test stand, Andromeda Sr., although fabrication was halted due to the shutdown. Currently, we are redesigning the Phobos-3 with the intent of making it as light as possible while still being able to withstand the chamber pressure, as well as learning a lot about how to accurately run finite element analysis on the system as a whole.

  • Structures Sub-Team

    The Structures team has been working on the design of the rocket airframe, aerodynamic analysis, and development of composites manufacturing techniques that will be used to build the main tube of the rocket. We have narrowed down our design to use a vacuum-bagged unidirectional carbon fiber layup for the main tubes, and have built our first test tube to be tested in Olin’s Material Science laboratory when we return to campus. We designed a fiberglass nose cone with a Von Karman profile in order to allow RF transparency and minimal transonic drag. We are currently designing a balsa wood and carbon fiber composite fin which uses a supercritical, symmetrical airfoil. The carbon skin of each fin will flare into the body tube to reduce interference drag. We are currently designing a telescoping body tube coupler to interact with the recovery system. Our next steps are to complete a full 3D model of the rocket including each subsystem in order to reduce design interference, and to begin finite element analysis on each portion of the rocket to make sure it can withstand the high loads it will experience in flight.

  • Avionics Sub-Team

    Over the 2019-2020 academic year, the Avionics team has observed a huge growth in team size - we now have 10 total members, up from 5, working on designing the main flight computer, sensing, apogee detection, onboard recovery system, the battery management system, and the electro-mechanical integration of the electronic hardware spatially inside of the rocket. We have fabricated and tested the main flight computer which has a microcontroller board, a long-range radio module, an altimeter, an accelerometer, and a GPS measurement unit all working together to track in-flight motion. The program that runs on this printed circuit board is currently under testing and should be up and running as soon as the team is able to return to Olin’s campus. The avionics system has been designed to activate a series of solenoids and heat up a nichrome wire system which is responsible for parachute deployment as the rocket hits apogee. The battery management system will serve the power needs for all of the avionics’ electronics, along with a backup battery pack as a power fail-safe. Quite recently, we have appointed a team for Research and Development in avionics - the members will be looking at ways to better our current system in order to work more reliably, as well as to design more learning opportunities for future members. Our year-end goals include testing the remaining avionics systems (recovery and battery management) and the flight computer software for early edge-case detection. We have also begun documentation for this past year and given initial thoughts on the timeline and budget goals for the upcoming academic year.

  • Recovery Sub-Team

    The recovery team has been working on a prototype of the Phoenix III recovery system. We have moved to using a pressurized air tank, a solenoid and a pneumatic piston to separate the rocket and deploy the drogue chute. The drogue chute will slow down the rocket from apogee to 1500ft and then we are going to deploy the main parachute. The main parachute will be restrained inside the rocket with a nylon cord and will be deployed byburning through the cord using a nichrome wire. After building a prototype we successfully proved that the piston is strong enough to break through the pins holding the rocket together, and that the nichrome wire can burn the nylon cord. Our next step is to redesign some aspects of the system and machine the flight hardware. We are looking forward to doing drop tests next semester and flying the system in competition!
